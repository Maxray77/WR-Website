## 2. The Origin Story

In the late 1990s, brothers Nadeem and Saud found an injured Black Kite on the streets of Old Delhi. When they took the bird to veterinary hospitals, they were turned away — "We do not treat carnivorous birds." Rather than accept this, the brothers began teaching themselves wildlife rehabilitation from scratch.

What started at home — with no formal training, no funding, and no infrastructure — grew into a 24/7 rescue operation that would eventually be featured in an Oscar-nominated documentary seen around the world.

The Hashmi brothers' facility, tucked into a cramped basement in Old Delhi, became a refuge for thousands of birds a year. Their self-taught surgical techniques for repairing wings damaged by kite strings have since been adopted by wildlife rehabilitators worldwide.

### The Black Kite Crisis
The Black Kite (*Milvus migrans*) is Delhi's most abundant raptor, with an estimated 20,000+ birds in the city. It is the cornerstone of Delhi's ecological balance — a natural urban scavenger. Approximately 80% of all intake at Wildlife Rescue are Black Kites, overwhelmingly injured by manja (glass-coated synthetic kite string used during festival season). Wildlife Rescue pioneered surgical techniques to repair severed tendons and fractured bones in raptor wings, with outcomes that were previously considered impossible.

## 7. Key Threats to Delhi's Birds

1. **Manja (glass-coated kite string)** — The #1 threat. Synthetic manja cuts through feathers, skin, and bone. Banned in 2017 but widely sold illegally. Injuries peak during Makar Sankranti (January), Basant Panchami, and Independence Day (August).

2. **Electrocution** — High-voltage power lines, especially during monsoon when birds' wet feathers conduct electricity. A leading cause of death and severe wing burns.

3. **Vehicle collisions** — Urbanization pushes birds into traffic. Low-flying raptors hunting near roads are especially vulnerable.

4. **Poisoning** — Rodenticides accumulate up the food chain. Diclofenac (banned but still present) has decimated vulture populations across South Asia by 99%.

5. **Habitat loss** — Rapid urbanization destroys nesting trees. Delhi loses hundreds of old trees annually to construction.

6. **Window strikes** — Modern glass buildings are invisible to birds. A growing cause of injury in new commercial districts.

## 8. Medical Specialties

Wildlife Rescue developed world-class techniques for raptor rehabilitation, entirely through self-teaching and iteration:

- **Wing fracture repair** — Including complex comminuted fractures requiring internal fixation
- **Tendon reconstruction** — Repairing tendons severed by manja, restoring full flight
- **Feather imping** — Grafting donor feathers to restore flight capability before natural moult
- **Eye injury treatment** — Specialized care for corneal injuries and eye infections
- **Malnutrition rehabilitation** — Gradual refeeding protocols for critically emaciated birds
- **Burn treatment** — From electrocution injuries
- **Toxicology management** — Treatment for lead and organophosphate poisoning

**Notable medical milestone:** Surgical techniques developed at Wildlife Rescue for repairing kite-string injuries are now referenced in rehabilitation literature and used by practitioners internationally.

## 14. Flagship Rescue Stories

### Kiran the Black Kite
Found entangled in manja string on a Delhi rooftop with severe lacerations through the wing membrane and a fractured radius. After six weeks of intensive care including surgical tendon repair and physiotherapy, Kiran was released back into the Delhi skies.

### Noor the Barn Owl
A juvenile Barn Owl fell from her nest during a storm. Malnourished, dehydrated, and suffering from minor injuries, she was tube-fed and nursed back to full health over four weeks. She was released at dusk near her original nest site.

### Sultan the Egyptian Vulture
An Egyptian Vulture — a critically endangered species — was found with a fractured humerus from a collision. This rare species, whose global population has declined by over 80% in South Asia due to diclofenac poisoning, required months of specialist care and is one of only a handful of Egyptian Vultures to have been successfully rehabilitated in India.

## 15. The Vulture Crisis

South Asia's vulture population has collapsed by over 99% since the 1980s — one of the fastest declines of any bird species in recorded history. The culprit: **diclofenac**, an anti-inflammatory drug given to livestock that is lethal to vultures when they feed on carcasses.

Wildlife Rescue works with all species of vultures found in Delhi's skies:
- Egyptian Vulture *(Neophron percnopterus)* — Endangered
- White-rumped Vulture *(Gyps bengalensis)* — Critically Endangered
- Long-billed Vulture *(Gyps indicus)* — Critically Endangered
- Himalayan Griffon *(Gyps himalayensis)*
- Eurasian Griffon *(Gyps fulvus)*
- Cinereous Vulture *(Aegypius monachus)*
- Bearded Vulture / Lammergeier *(Gypaetus barbatus)*

Vultures are essential for public health — a single vulture carcass can consume 2 kg of meat per feeding, preventing the spread of disease. Their collapse has been linked to rising rabies deaths in some areas as feral dog populations increase to fill the scavenger niche.
